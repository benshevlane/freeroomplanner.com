Planning Your IKEA PAX Wardrobe in the UK: Everything You Need to Know

If you've ever spent an evening wrestling with the IKEA PAX planner UK tool online, you'll know it can be both incredibly useful and surprisingly frustrating. You're trying to visualise your new wardrobe in the context of your whole bedroom — but the official tool only lets you design the wardrobe itself, not the room around it. That's where a free, browser-based room planner like the one at freeroomplanner.com comes in to fill the gap.

In this guide, we'll walk you through everything you need to know about planning a PAX wardrobe for a UK home — from measuring your space correctly and understanding PAX dimensions, to using a free floor planner to make sure your new wardrobe actually fits before you order a single flat pack.

What Is the IKEA PAX System?

The IKEA PAX system is one of the most popular wardrobe solutions in UK homes. It's modular, customisable, and relatively affordable — which is why millions of people turn to it every year when planning a bedroom renovation. The system works around a set of standardised frame widths and heights, which you combine with doors, internal organisers, drawers, and shelving to create your ideal storage solution.

Standard PAX Frame Sizes in the UK

Understanding the standard sizes is essential before you begin planning. In the UK, PAX frames are available in the following widths:

  • 50cm wide – ideal for tight spaces or end units
  • 75cm wide – the most versatile width for single wardrobes
  • 100cm wide – great for larger built-in looks

Heights come in two options:

  • 201cm – standard ceiling height
  • 236cm – for rooms with higher ceilings

The depth is typically 58cm (or 35cm for a shallower version). These measurements are critical when you're planning your bedroom layout, because a row of PAX wardrobes along a wall can easily eat into your usable floor space by 60cm or more once doors are factored in.

Why the Official IKEA PAX Planner Isn't Always Enough

The official IKEA PAX planner UK tool is a great starting point. It allows you to choose frames, select doors, and pick interior fittings. However, it has some well-known limitations:

  • It doesn't show your wardrobe in the context of your full room layout
  • You can't add furniture like beds, bedside tables, or dressing tables to see how everything fits together
  • It doesn't help you check clearance space — for example, whether your bedroom door will still open fully once the wardrobe is in place
  • It can be slow and occasionally buggy on older devices or browsers
  • It doesn't support floor plan export in a format you can share easily with a fitter or contractor

This is exactly why many homeowners use a separate room planner alongside the IKEA tool — to get the full picture before committing to a purchase.

How to Use freeroomplanner.com to Plan Your PAX Wardrobe Layout

The free bedroom planner at freeroomplanner.com lets you draw your room to scale, place furniture items, and visualise your full bedroom layout — all in your browser, with no sign-up or download required. Here's how to use it effectively when planning a PAX wardrobe installation.

Step 1: Draw Your Room Walls to Scale

Start by drawing the outline of your bedroom. The tool uses a snap-to-grid system with 10cm increments, which makes it easy to be precise. Measure your room carefully before you start — note the width and length of every wall, and don't forget to mark any alcoves, chimney breasts, or recesses that might affect where your wardrobe can go.

Add your doors and windows too. This is crucial when planning a PAX installation, because sliding doors need clearance on one side, and hinged doors need a full swing arc of space in front of them.

Step 2: Place Your Existing Furniture

Before you place the wardrobe, drag in your existing bedroom furniture — your bed, bedside tables, chest of drawers, and any other pieces you're keeping. The planner includes over 30 furniture items you can drag, resize, and reposition. This gives you a realistic view of how much wall space you actually have available.

Step 3: Represent Your PAX Wardrobe as a Rectangle

Since PAX wardrobes are essentially rectangular boxes, you can easily represent them in the floor planner using a furniture block sized to match your planned configuration. For example:

  • A 3-unit PAX run using 75cm + 75cm + 100cm frames = 250cm wide, 58cm deep
  • A 2-unit run using 100cm + 100cm frames = 200cm wide, 58cm deep

Resize the furniture block to these dimensions using the live measurement display — the planner shows measurements in metres, centimetres, and feet, so you can match them precisely to IKEA's specifications.

Step 4: Check Clearance and Circulation Space

This is the step that the official IKEA PAX planner UK tool simply doesn't offer. Once your wardrobe block is in place, you can visually check:

  • Whether there's enough space to stand in front of the wardrobe and open the doors (hinged doors typically need 58–65cm of clearance in front)
  • Whether the bedroom door still has a clear swing path
  • Whether there's comfortable circulation space around the bed
  • Whether any radiators or plug sockets will be blocked

Getting this right at the planning stage can save you from a very expensive and frustrating installation mistake.

Tips for Getting the Most Out of Your PAX Wardrobe

Planning the layout is just one part of the process. Here are some practical tips to help you get the best result from your IKEA PAX installation in a UK bedroom.

Measure Twice, Order Once

UK houses, especially older ones, rarely have perfectly square rooms or plumb-straight walls. Before you finalise your PAX configuration, measure your wall at three different heights — at the floor, at mid-height, and at ceiling level. If the measurements vary by more than a centimetre or two, you may need to account for this with filler panels or by having the frames fitted slightly away from the wall.

Consider Ceiling Height

The standard PAX height of 201cm works in most UK homes, but if you're fitting the wardrobes in a room with a lower ceiling, you might need to check there's enough room above the frame for the door runners. For rooms with higher ceilings — common in period properties — the 236cm frame can create a dramatic, built-in look. You can also add a top box or cornice above the standard 201cm frame to fill the gap to the ceiling.

Think About Internal Organisation

The internal fittings are where PAX can really earn its value. Think carefully about what you'll be storing before you order:

  • Long hanging space for dresses, coats, and suits
  • Short hanging space (often doubled up) for shirts, jackets, and folded trousers
  • Shelving for folded items, shoes, and accessories
  • Drawers (PAX AULI or KOMPLEMENT) for underwear, socks, and smaller items
  • Pull-out shoe racks if you have a large collection

Matching Your Doors to the Room

PAX doors come in a huge range of finishes, from plain white to mirrored to glass-panelled designs. In smaller bedrooms, mirrored doors are a popular choice in UK homes because they reflect light and make the room feel larger. Bear this in mind when you're doing your room planning — the reflective effect can significantly change how a small bedroom feels.
