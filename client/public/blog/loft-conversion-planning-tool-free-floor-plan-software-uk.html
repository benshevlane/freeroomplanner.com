Loft Conversion Planning Tool: Free Floor Plan Software for UK Extensions

2026-05-18 · 7 min read Try Free Room Planner free

Planning a loft conversion without proper floor plans is like building a house on quicksand. Most homeowners dive into their conversion with rough sketches and vague ideas, only to face expensive surprises when builders point out overlooked head heights, awkward stair positions, or impossible furniture placement.

This guide shows you how to plan your loft conversion properly using free floor plan software, ensuring your extension works before you break ground.

What Makes a Good Loft Conversion Planning Tool

Your loft conversion planning tool needs to handle the unique challenges of roof space conversions. Standard room planners often fall short because they don't account for sloped ceilings, dormer windows, or stair positioning.

Essential Features for Loft Planning

Accurate measurements — Every wall, window, and structural element needs precise dimensions. A 10cm error in head height calculations can make the difference between a comfortable bedroom and a space you can't stand up in.

Angled wall support — Your tool must handle sloped ceilings and irregular shapes. Most lofts aren't perfect rectangles.

Furniture testing — Place beds, wardrobes, and desks to check they actually fit under sloped areas before construction begins.

Stair integration — Plan where your new stairs will go and how they impact both floors.

No download requirements — You need something that works immediately, not another software package to learn.

Step-by-Step Loft Conversion Planning Process

Step 1: Measure Your Existing Loft Space

Start with accurate measurements of your current loft. You'll need:

  • Overall floor dimensions (length and width)
  • Ceiling height at the ridge (highest point)
  • Ceiling height at the eaves (lowest usable point)
  • Positions of existing structural beams
  • Window locations and sizes
  • Chimney or tank positions

Use a digital tape measure for accuracy. Round measurements to the nearest centimetre — precision matters when you're working with tight head heights.

Step 2: Check Minimum Height Requirements

UK building regulations require a minimum ceiling height of 2.3 metres over at least half the floor area. Mark this on your floor plan to identify usable space.

Areas under 1.5 metres are typically unusable for anything except storage. Areas between 1.5 and 2.3 metres work for built-in furniture or seating areas.

Step 3: Plan Your Stair Position

Your new stairs need roughly three metres of straight-line floor space on the level below. Mark potential stair positions on both your existing floor plan and your new loft plan.

Consider:

  • Access from existing hallways or landing areas
  • Impact on rooms below
  • Head height at the top of the stairs
  • Fire escape requirements (especially for bedroom conversions)

Step 4: Design Your Loft Layout

Open your free floor plan software and create your loft outline using your measurements. Most browser-based tools like Free Room Planner let you draw irregular shapes for sloped ceilings.

Add structural elements first:

  • Existing beams and supports
  • Chimney breasts
  • Water tanks or services
  • New structural elements (if planned)

Plan room divisions second:

  • Bedroom areas
  • Bathroom space (if included)
  • Storage areas
  • Circulation space

Step 5: Test Furniture Placement

This step separates successful conversions from disappointing ones. Add furniture to your floor plan:

  • Beds — Standard double beds need two metres of clear floor space in front
  • Wardrobes — Account for door swing and drawer access
  • Desks — Check you can fit a chair and have elbow room
  • Storage — Built-in storage often works better than freestanding furniture

Move furniture around digitally until you find arrangements that work. Save multiple layout options to compare.

Step 6: Plan Services and Access

Electrical planning:

  • Light switch positions (accessible from bed and entry point)
  • Socket locations for lamps, charging, and equipment
  • Ceiling light positions that work with sloped ceilings

Heating planning:

  • Radiator positions (often work well under windows)
  • Ventilation requirements
  • Insulation considerations

Plumbing (if adding a bathroom):

  • Connect to existing soil pipes where possible
  • Plan drainage falls
  • Water supply access

Common Loft Conversion Planning Mistakes

Ignoring Head Height Zones

Many homeowners focus on total floor area and forget about usable space. A 50-square-metre loft with poor head height distribution feels smaller than a 35-square-metre space planned properly.

Mark head height zones on your floor plan:

  • Green zones (2.3m+): Full-height furniture, walking areas
  • Amber zones (1.8-2.3m): Seating, low furniture, children's areas
  • Red zones (<1.8m): Storage only

Poor Stair Planning

Stairs eat floor space on both levels. Many conversions fail because homeowners plan the loft first, then discover the stairs don't fit properly below.

Plan stairs early in your process. Mark the stair footprint on your existing floor plan before finalising your loft layout.

Overlooking Storage Needs

Loft conversions often reduce existing storage space. Plan replacement storage into your new layout from the beginning.

Built-in storage works well in low-height areas under eaves. Design these storage areas into your floor plan rather than adding them later.

Window and Light Planning Errors

Existing loft windows rarely provide enough light for living spaces. Plan new window positions early — they affect both interior layout and external planning permission.

Roof windows (skylights) need clear floor space below for access and furniture placement. Dormer windows add floor space but require structural work.

Planning Permission and Building Regulations

Your floor plans serve double duty — interior planning and official applications.

Permitted Development Rights

Many loft conversions fall under permitted development, avoiding full planning permission. Your floor plans need to show:

  • Overall house footprint remains unchanged
  • New windows don't overlook neighbouring properties
  • Volume additions stay within permitted limits

Building Regulations Approval

All loft conversions need building regulations approval. Your floor plans must demonstrate:

  • Adequate ceiling heights in living areas
  • Proper fire escape routes
  • Structural adequacy (requires structural engineer input)
  • Insulation and ventilation planning

Advanced Loft Conversion Planning Tips

Multi-Purpose Space Planning

Loft conversions work best when rooms serve multiple functions. Plan spaces that adapt throughout the day:

  • Home office by day, guest room by night — Sofa bed and fold-away desk
  • Exercise area and storage — Equipment that stores flat against walls
  • Children's playroom and study space — Zoned areas for different activities

Built-In Furniture Integration

Custom built-ins maximise awkward loft spaces better than standard furniture. Plan these into your floor plan:

  • Under-eaves storage with angled doors
  • Desk areas built into dormer recesses
  • Bed platforms that create storage space below
  • Wardrobe runs following the roofline

Future-Proofing Your Conversion

Plan for changing needs:

  • Flexible room divisions using partition walls rather than structural changes
  • Extra electrical capacity for future equipment
  • Plumbing rough-in for potential future bathroom additions
  • Stair width that accommodates furniture moving

Coordinating with Professionals

Your floor plan becomes the communication hub for your conversion project.

Builder Briefings

Share detailed floor plans with potential builders during the quotation process. Clear plans reduce misunderstandings and help builders price accurately.

Include:

  • Room dimensions and layouts
  • Electrical and plumbing requirements
  • Built-in furniture specifications
  • Access and delivery considerations

Structural Engineer Coordination

Structural engineers need your floor plans to calculate load requirements and beam specifications. Share your layout early so structural solutions integrate with your room planning.

Planning Officer Discussions

For conversions requiring planning permission, clear floor plans help planning officers understand your proposal quickly. Include context plans showing the conversion's relationship to existing rooms.

Cost Planning Integration

Use your floor plan for accurate cost estimation.

Area-Based Costs

  • Flooring — Calculate square metreage from your floor plan
  • Insulation — Roof and wall areas for material quantities
  • Decorating — Wall and ceiling areas for paint and wallpaper

Fixed-Cost Elements

  • Stairs — Position affects complexity and cost
  • Windows — Number and type from your plan
  • Electrics — Circuit requirements from your layout
  • Plumbing — Distance from existing services
