Understanding the B&Q Kitchen Units Range

B&Q offers one of the most accessible kitchen ranges on the UK market, with units available through their Cooke & Lewis and GoodHome ranges. Before you plan your space, it helps to understand the standard sizing conventions their units follow, since these will dictate how your floor plan comes together.

Standard Unit Widths

  • 300mm – Narrow base or wall units, ideal for filling gaps or housing pull-out larders
  • 400mm – Good for small spaces or additional drawer units
  • 500mm – A common mid-size for base cabinets and wall units
  • 600mm – The most popular width for base units, dishwashers, and ovens
  • 800mm and 1000mm – Wider units used for sink bases or large drawer packs

Standard Heights and Depths

B&Q base units typically stand at 720mm tall (before worktop), with a standard depth of 570mm for base cabinets. Wall units come in heights of 360mm, 570mm, or 900mm, depending on the range and style. Tall larder and tower units usually measure 1970mm or 2150mm in height.

Knowing these dimensions is crucial when you're using a floor planner, because you'll be working in real-world measurements to check whether your chosen units actually fit the space you have.

Popular Kitchen Layouts and How They Work With B&Q Units

B&Q kitchen units are available in enough widths and configurations to suit almost any layout style. Here's how the most popular kitchen shapes translate into practical planning decisions.

Single Wall (Galley) Kitchens

A single wall layout runs all units along one wall. It's common in flats, studio apartments, and narrow kitchen spaces. When planning this layout with B&Q units, the main challenge is maximising storage within a limited linear run. Consider a combination of 600mm base units, full-height larder units at one end, and 900mm tall wall units to make the most of vertical space.

L-Shaped Kitchens

One of the most popular UK kitchen layouts, the L-shape uses two adjoining walls. B&Q's corner base unit options (including blind corner and carousel units) are designed specifically for this configuration. In your floor plan, pay close attention to the corner itself — poorly planned corners waste significant space and can make nearby drawers difficult to open.

U-Shaped Kitchens

U-shaped layouts wrap units around three walls, offering the greatest amount of storage and worktop space. When planning a U-shaped kitchen with B&Q kitchen units, check that the minimum clearance between opposing unit runs is at least 1.2 metres (ideally 1.5 metres) to allow two people to work comfortably and for dishwasher and oven doors to open fully.

Island Kitchens

Kitchen islands have become a fixture in UK home renovations. B&Q offer island-specific base units as well as standard units that can be configured island-style. When planning an island, allow a minimum of 900mm clearance on all sides — 1050mm or more if the island will also serve as a dining area.

Tips for Getting the Most From Your Kitchen Plan

Experienced kitchen designers and fitters consistently cite the same planning mistakes. Here's how to avoid them when working with B&Q kitchen units:

  • Account for appliance depth. Many integrated appliances — particularly fridge-freezers and range cookers — are deeper than standard 570mm base units. Mark these accurately in your floor plan to avoid worktop overhangs or awkward gaps.
  • Plan your extraction route early. Cooker hood placement depends on ceiling height and the distance to an external wall or ducting route. Get this confirmed before you finalise your layout.
  • Don't forget the plinth and cornice. B&Q units are supplied without plinths and cornicing in many ranges. Factor these finishing elements into your height calculations, particularly if you're planning tall units to ceiling height.
  • Check door hinge clearance. Every base unit and wall unit door needs clearance space to open without hitting an adjacent cabinet, a wall, or an appliance. The floor planner makes it straightforward to spot these conflicts before they happen.
  • Leave room for the fitter. Some homeowners plan a kitchen so tightly that there's no practical way to install it. Allow a little flexibility, particularly around end panels and filler strips — B&Q supply these precisely for this reason.
