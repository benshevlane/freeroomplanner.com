B&Q Kitchen Units: How to Plan Your Perfect Kitchen Before You Buy

Published 2026-03-26

Buying a new kitchen is one of the biggest investments you'll make in your home. Whether you're eyeing up the Imandra range or browsing the latest Cooke & Lewis collection, choosing your B&Q kitchen units is only half the battle. The real challenge — and the part most homeowners underestimate — is planning the layout. Get it wrong and you could end up with units that don't fit, worktops that clash with your plumbing, or a kitchen that simply doesn't work the way you imagined.

The good news? You don't need to pay for expensive design software or wait for a B&Q home planner appointment to start visualising your space. In this guide, we'll walk you through exactly how to plan a kitchen that works, how to measure up for B&Q kitchen units, and how to use a free online tool to design your layout before you spend a single penny.

Why Planning Your Kitchen Layout Matters Before You Buy

It's tempting to browse the B&Q website, fall in love with a range, and start adding items to your basket. But ordering kitchen units without a solid floor plan is a recipe for expensive mistakes. Here's why planning first always pays off:

  • Avoid costly returns: Kitchen units are bulky, heavy, and often non-refundable once assembled. Ordering the wrong sizes can leave you with units that don't fit and no easy way to return them.
  • Optimise your workflow: A well-planned kitchen follows the classic work triangle — the relationship between your hob, sink, and fridge. Poor positioning means more steps, more frustration, and a kitchen that feels awkward to use every day.
  • Spot problems early: Windows, doors, radiators, and boilers all affect where units can go. A floor plan helps you identify conflicts before they become real-world headaches.
  • Save money on fitting: Kitchen fitters charge by the hour (and the complication). The more accurate your plan, the smoother the installation, and the less you'll pay.

Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

Before you start planning, it helps to understand how B&Q kitchen units are sized. Like most UK kitchen suppliers, B&Q follows standard cabinet dimensions. Getting familiar with these will make your planning process much more straightforward.

Standard Base Unit Dimensions

  • Height: 720mm (excluding worktop, which adds roughly 38mm)
  • Depth: 570mm (to allow for a standard 600mm deep worktop with a small overhang)
  • Width options: 150mm, 300mm, 400mm, 450mm, 500mm, 600mm, 800mm, and 1000mm

Standard Wall Unit Dimensions

  • Height: 360mm, 570mm, or 720mm
  • Depth: 300mm
  • Width options: 300mm, 400mm, 450mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 1000mm

Tall Units and Larder Cabinets

  • Height: 1970mm or 2150mm
  • Depth: 570mm
  • Width options: 300mm, 500mm, or 600mm

How to Measure Your Kitchen for B&Q Units

Accurate measurements are the foundation of any successful kitchen project. Rushing this stage is one of the most common — and costly — mistakes homeowners make. Follow these steps carefully.

Step 1: Sketch Your Room Shape

Before you pick up a tape measure, grab a pencil and paper and sketch the rough shape of your kitchen. Note the position of every door, window, and fixed feature such as the boiler, soil pipe, gas supply point, and any structural columns or alcoves. You don't need it to be perfect — you're just creating a reference for your measurements.

Step 2: Measure the Walls

Measure each wall from corner to corner at worktop height (approximately 900mm from the floor). Don't just measure along the floor — walls can bow or be uneven, and worktop height is what matters for unit planning. Record every measurement in millimetres for accuracy.

Step 3: Measure Doors and Windows

For each door and window, measure:

  • The width of the opening
  • The distance from the nearest corner to the edge of the frame
  • For windows: the height from the floor to the window sill, and from the floor to the top of the frame

These measurements will tell you how much usable wall space you have for base and wall units, and whether you need to leave clearance for doors to swing open.

Step 4: Note Fixed Services

Mark the position of your existing plumbing (sink waste, cold water supply, hot water supply), gas supply, and electrical sockets. Moving these adds significant cost to a kitchen renovation, so planning around them where possible will save you money.

Step 5: Check for Obstructions

Look for radiators, boilers, extractor ducts, and anything else that might prevent a unit from sitting flush against the wall. Measure the height and width of each obstruction and mark it on your sketch.

Planning Your Kitchen Layout: The Key Principles

With your measurements in hand, you can start thinking about your kitchen layout. There are several classic kitchen layouts, each suited to different room shapes and sizes.

Single-Wall Kitchen

All units run along one wall. Ideal for narrow kitchens or open-plan spaces where the kitchen is just one element of a larger room. Works well with B&Q kitchen units in standard widths laid end to end.

Galley Kitchen

Units on two parallel walls. Efficient use of space and great for the work triangle, but can feel cramped if the space between the two runs is less than 1200mm. Aim for at least 1000mm minimum clearance, though 1200mm is more comfortable.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Units running along two adjacent walls. One of the most popular layouts in UK homes — versatile, efficient, and works well in both small and medium-sized kitchens. The corner unit requires careful planning (a corner base unit or a carousel unit typically fits here).

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls. Maximises storage and worktop space, but requires a wider room. Ideal for families who need lots of prep and storage space.

Island Kitchen

Any of the above layouts with a freestanding island unit in the centre. Requires significant floor space — typically at least 1000mm clearance on all sides of the island. Check whether B&Q's island unit options work with your chosen range.

Tips for Buying B&Q Kitchen Units

Once your layout is finalised and you know exactly which units you need, here are some practical tips to make the buying process as smooth as possible.

Check Stock Before You Commit

B&Q kitchen ranges can go in and out of stock, and some units are only available in certain sizes. Before you finalise your design, check online or in-store that every unit you need is available in the size you require.

Order Spares for Hinges and Drawer Runners

These small components are often the first to need replacing, and having a few spares from the outset means you won't need to track down the exact part years later when the range may have been discontinued.

Factor in Filler Pieces and Plinths

Kitchens rarely fit perfectly from one wall to another. Filler pieces and end panels bridge the gaps and give a professional, finished look. Make sure you include these in your order.

Don't Forget the Worktop

B&Q offers a wide range of worktops to complement their kitchen units. Measure your worktop runs carefully — including around any hob cutouts or sink cutouts — and add a small amount for cutting waste.

Book Your Delivery Slot Early

Kitchen units are large and heavy, and delivery slots can book up quickly, especially around bank holidays. Once your order is placed, book your delivery slot as soon as possible to align with your fitter's schedule.

Sharing Your Plan With Your Kitchen Fitter

One of the most practical benefits of drawing your plan digitally is being able to share it. Whether you're using a local kitchen fitter or a larger company, giving them a clear floor plan at the quoting stage helps enormously. It allows them to:

  • Give you a more accurate quote based on the actual scope of work
  • Identify any potential installation challenges in advance
  • Confirm the order of work (plumbing, electrical, units, worktops)
  • Reduce the chance of surprises on installation day

Simply export your finished plan as a PNG from our kitchen planner and send it across. It's a small step that can save significant time and money further down the line.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the standard sizes for B&Q kitchen units?

B&Q kitchen units follow standard UK cabinet sizing. Base units are typically 720mm tall and 570mm deep, with widths ranging from 150mm to 1000mm. Wall units come in heights of 360mm, 570mm, or 720mm and are generally 300mm deep. Tall larder units are available in 1970mm and 2150mm heights. Always check the specific product listing for exact dimensions, as they can vary between ranges.

Can I plan my kitchen online before visiting a B&Q store?

Absolutely — and it's highly recommended. Using a free tool like the kitchen planner at freeroomplanner.com lets you draw your room accurately, experiment with different layouts, and work out exactly which units you need before you set foot in a store. Going in with a clear plan saves time and helps you avoid making expensive impulse decisions.

How much space do I need between kitchen units on opposite walls?

The minimum recommended clearance between opposing kitchen units is 1000mm, though 1200mm is more comfortable for everyday use and much better if two people will be working in the kitchen simultaneously. In a galley layout, less than 1000mm will feel very cramped and may also cause issues with appliance doors opening fully.

Do I need to hire a designer to plan a B&Q kitchen?

No — many homeowners successfully plan their own kitchens using free online tools. B&Q itself offers a planning service in-store, but you can absolutely do this yourself, especially if you take accurate measurements and use a proper floor planning tool. A self-planned kitchen with clear drawings can be just as successful as a professionally designed one, and you'll have a much deeper understanding of your space as a result.
