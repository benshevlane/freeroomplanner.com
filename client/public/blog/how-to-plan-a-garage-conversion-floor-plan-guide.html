How to Plan a Garage Conversion: Step-by-Step Floor Plan Guide

Most people never get past that thought. Not because the project is too complicated — but because the planning stage feels like a wall. Where do you even start?

What Can You Actually Do With a Garage?

Before you draw a single line, it helps to know what you're converting to. The good news: garages are one of the most flexible spaces in any home. The bad news: that flexibility can make the decision feel harder than it needs to be.

The most popular garage conversion types are:

  • Home office — the number one use case, especially for remote workers who need a proper separation between work and home life
  • Extra bedroom or guest room — adds real value to your home and solves the problem of where people sleep when they visit
  • Home gym — no more expensive memberships; a dedicated workout space you can actually get to
  • Playroom or hobby studio — reclaims your living room without anyone feeling cramped
  • Utility room or laundry room — practical rather than glamorous, but genuinely useful
  • Open-plan kitchen extension — works best when the garage connects directly to an existing kitchen

The right choice depends on your household, your budget, and one practical constraint: the size of your garage.

Single Garage Conversions

A typical single garage runs around 5m x 2.5m to 5m x 3m — roughly the size of a large bedroom. That's enough space for a comfortable home office, a single bedroom, a compact gym, or a utility room.

What it isn't is enormous. Furniture placement matters a lot in a single garage conversion, because every square metre counts. That's exactly why drawing the layout before you start is so useful — you can check whether your desk, sofa, or bed actually fits before anyone lifts a hammer.

Double Garage Conversions

A double garage typically measures around 5m x 5.5m or larger — closer to 27–30 square metres in total. That opens up much more ambitious options: split the space into two rooms (a gym and a utility room, for example), create a generous open-plan studio, or build a proper guest suite with an en-suite bathroom.

Double garages also allow for more interesting layouts. You have room for a partition wall, a hallway, and separate entrance points. This is where a floor plan becomes especially valuable — double the space means double the decisions.

Check These Things Before You Draw Anything

A floor plan is only as good as the information behind it. Spend an afternoon in your garage before you open any planning tool, and check these five things.

  1. Ceiling height — most garages have a ceiling height of around 2.1m to 2.4m. Habitable rooms in the UK typically require a minimum of 2.1m, but taller is always better. Check yours with a tape measure.
  2. Floor condition — garage floors are often uneven, sloped toward a drain, or sitting directly on bare concrete. Any of these can affect your insulation and flooring choices, and potentially the room height once a raised floor is fitted.
  3. Existing drainage — if you're planning a bathroom, utility room, or kitchen-adjacent conversion, note where any existing drainage points or inspection covers are. Moving them is expensive.
  4. Natural light — where does light currently enter the space? The garage door opening is an obvious one, but check for any side windows too. Natural light heavily influences where you'll position a desk, bed, or seating area.
  5. Attached vs detached — an attached garage shares a wall with your home, which simplifies heating and electrical connections. A detached garage is a separate structure with its own set of access and utility challenges.
Tip: Take photos of each wall, the ceiling, and the floor while you're in there. You'll refer back to them constantly when you're drawing your layout.

For anything involving structural walls, load-bearing elements, or foundations, speak to a structural engineer. That's outside the scope of this guide — and outside the scope of any floor plan tool.

How to Measure Your Garage Correctly

Accurate measurements are the foundation of any usable floor plan. Get them wrong here, and you'll be redesigning midway through the build — which costs money and time you don't need to spend.

You need: a tape measure (a laser measure is faster and more accurate if you have one), a notepad or your phone, and ideally a second person to hold the other end.

What to Record and How

Work around the space in a logical order:

  1. Overall width and depth — measure wall to wall at floor level. Do this twice: once at the front of the space and once at the back. Walls aren't always perfectly parallel.
  2. Ceiling height — measure floor to ceiling at the centre of the room and at each corner. Note any beams or obstructions.
  3. Door and window positions — measure from the nearest wall corner to each edge of every door or window opening. Record the width and height of each opening.
  4. Alcoves, pillars, and step-downs — any protrusion or recess needs its own measurements. These are easy to forget and expensive to discover mid-build.
  5. Garage door opening — even if you're blocking it up, record the exact width and height. This becomes your main window or entrance in most conversions.

Common Measuring Mistakes to Sidestep

  • Measuring at head height instead of floor level — walls taper and lean more than you think. Always measure at floor level for dimensions you'll use in a floor plan.
  • Forgetting the thickness of existing walls — if there's an internal skin of plasterboard already fitted, measure to the face of that, not the raw brick behind it.
  • Ignoring the garage door reveal — the frame around a garage door opening often projects inward by 10–15cm. That affects how wide your new entrance or window can be.
Tip: Write all your measurements on a rough hand sketch before opening any planning tool. Even a scrappy diagram saves a lot of back-and-forth between the garage and your screen.

Understanding Garage Conversion Floor Plan Layouts

Once you have your measurements, the next step is deciding how to use the space. Garage conversion floor plan ideas fall into a few clear categories, and knowing which one fits your goals will save you hours of trial and error.

Open-Plan vs Partitioned Layouts

An open-plan layout keeps the entire footprint as one room. This works well for single garages where every square metre matters — a home office, gym, or studio benefits from feeling as spacious as possible. Open-plan layouts are also simpler and cheaper to build, since you're not adding internal walls.

A partitioned layout divides the space into two or more distinct areas. This is the natural choice for double garages, where you might want a home office on one side and a utility room on the other, or a gym with a separate shower room. Partitioned layouts need careful planning because the partition wall eats into usable floor area and must be positioned to give both rooms a workable shape.

For smaller single garages, a semi-partition — a half-height wall or a freestanding shelving unit — can define zones without shrinking either area.

Positioning Doors, Windows, and Natural Light

Door and window placement drives the whole layout. In most garage conversions, the garage door opening becomes either a large window, a set of French doors, or a full-length glazed wall. This is often the only substantial source of natural light, so where you position seating, a desk, or a bed relative to it matters a lot.

A practical rule: put the activity that benefits most from natural light closest to the main window. For a home office, that's your desk. For a bedroom, it might be a reading chair rather than the bed head, where direct morning light can be disruptive.

Think about door position too. Where does the conversion connect to your home — through the house or via an external entrance? That connection point affects traffic flow and where furniture can realistically go.

Garage Conversion Floor Plan Ideas by Room Type

Need inspiration before you start drawing? Here are layout ideas for the four most popular conversion types — with practical notes on what works and what to watch out for.

Home Office Layout Ideas

A home office is the most common garage conversion in the UK, and for good reason. Even a compact single garage gives you enough room for a proper desk setup, storage, and a meeting area if you need one.

The key layout decision is desk position. Face the desk toward the main window for natural light — but angle it slightly so you're not looking directly into bright sunlight during video calls. Position storage along the wall opposite the window to keep the light side of the room clear.

If the budget allows, a small en-suite WC transforms a home office into a fully self-contained work space. That's much easier to plan for from the start than to retrofit later — and a free home office layout planner lets you check whether the footprint works before you commit.

Bedroom or Guest Room Layout Ideas

A single garage converted to a bedroom typically accommodates a double bed, two bedside tables, a wardrobe, and a small desk — if you plan the layout carefully. The bed should sit on the wall furthest from the main entrance door, with a clear path of at least 70cm on the accessible side.

For a guest room with an en-suite, a double garage gives you the most flexibility. Position the shower room or wet room on the wall adjoining your main house — this puts it closest to your existing plumbing, which reduces cost. Use the bedroom planner to check furniture positions before you finalise the layout with your builder.

Home Gym Layout Ideas

Gyms need more floor clearance than almost any other room type. Equipment has fixed safety zones — a treadmill, for example, needs space behind it as well as to the sides. Draw your equipment to scale in the planner and check clearances before ordering anything.

Mirrors on one full wall visually double the space and help with form during workouts. Position them on the wall opposite the main window to avoid glare. If you're including a heavy barbell or power rack, note the ceiling height carefully — overhead lifts need more clearance than you might expect.

Playroom or Studio Layout Ideas

Playrooms and hobby studios benefit from open floor space above all else. Keep large furniture to the perimeter — shelving, a craft table, a sofa — and leave the centre of the room clear.

For a studio used for music, podcasting, or video, position the recording area away from the garage door wall, which is often the least well-insulated and the noisiest. A partition or acoustic treatment on that wall helps significantly, and it's much easier to plan for in a floor plan than to fix after the build.

Planning Permission and Building Regulations: What You Need to Know

This is the part of home renovation planning that trips people up most often — not because it's complicated, but because the rules vary by location and people assume the worst.

In the UK, most garage conversions fall under Permitted Development rights, which means you don't need full planning permission. However, there are exceptions: listed buildings, properties in conservation areas, and some new-build estates with specific covenants all require a formal application. The Planning Portal has a clear summary of when permission is and isn't required — check there first before assuming either way.

Regardless of planning permission, building regulations approval is required for virtually all garage conversions in the UK. This covers:

  • Insulation — walls, floor, and roof must meet current thermal standards
  • Ventilation — habitable rooms need adequate fresh air supply
  • Fire safety — escape routes, fire doors (where the conversion connects to the house), and smoke detection
  • Structural integrity — the conversion must be structurally sound (this is where a structural engineer comes in if any walls are being modified)
  • Electrical and plumbing work — must meet Part P and Part H regulations respectively

Your local authority building control department handles approvals. Many builders include managing this process in their quote — but it's worth asking explicitly.

In the US and Canada, planning and code requirements vary by state, province, and municipality. In most areas, a change of use from a garage to a habitable room requires a building permit. Check with your local planning department before starting any work.

How to Use Your Floor Plan When Talking to Builders

A floor plan changes the entire dynamic of a builder conversation. Instead of describing what you want and hoping they picture the same thing, you're showing them exactly what you mean. That removes ambiguity — and ambiguity is where cost overruns and disappointments come from.

When you share your floor plan, include:

  • Overall dimensions — width, depth, and ceiling height clearly marked
  • All door and window positions — with opening widths noted
  • Partition wall positions — if you're splitting the space
  • Any fixed elements — radiator positions, existing drainage points, electrical consumer units
  • A note on intended use — 'home office' or 'gym' tells a builder a lot about insulation, ventilation, and power requirements before they've asked a single question

Annotating a floor plan takes five minutes. Getting a misquoted job repriced mid-build takes much longer — and costs far more.

A good floor plan also makes it easier to get multiple quotes that are actually comparable. If every builder is pricing the same layout, you can compare their numbers directly. Without a plan, different builders may be pricing different interpretations of the same conversation.
