Tool comparison: 2D vs 3D features

2D tools show your room from above like a floor plan. They're faster, more accurate for measurements, and easier to hand to a contractor. 3D tools let you "walk" through the space and preview colours and furniture, which helps with visual decisions but adds time and complexity. Most renovators need a clean 2D plan first, then use 3D only if they want to picture the finished look.

What is the best free 3D home design software?

Planner 5D and Floorplanner are the strongest free 3D home design tools, offering large furniture catalogues, 3D walkthroughs, and free tiers. Autodesk Homestyler adds photo-realistic mock-ups. All three require an account, and their free versions limit some features. If you mainly want a shareable floor plan rather than a rendered room, a 2D planner is faster and simpler.

For pure visual planning — picturing paint, flooring, and furniture in 3D — these tools shine. SketchUp's free browser version goes further with detailed modelling, but it's built for people comfortable with 3D software.

How to draw a room plan accurately

Accuracy is the whole point. A plan that's a few centimetres off can mean a unit that won't close or a bath that won't fit. Follow these steps to get it right the first time.

  1. Measure every wall with a tape or laser measure, corner to corner.
  2. Note the height and width of doors, windows, and any alcoves.
  3. Mark fixed items — radiators, sockets, waste pipes, and the boiler.
  4. Open your room planning tool and set the scale or grid before you draw.
  5. Draw the walls first, using snap-to-grid so lines lock to real dimensions.
  6. Add doors and windows in their exact positions.
  7. Drop in furniture or units at their true sizes to check fit and clearance.
  8. Double-check clearances — 60cm minimum walkways, appliance door swings, seating gaps.
  9. Label key measurements directly on the plan.
  10. Export the finished plan as a clean image.

For tight spaces, small details decide everything. Our comparison of kitchen peninsula versus island space requirements shows how clearances change what fits.

How to share your plan with contractors

A plan only works if your contractor can read it. Do this before you send:

  1. Export in a common format — a PNG or PDF opens on any device without special software.
  2. Annotate the essentials — wall lengths, ceiling height, door and window positions, and where services (water, gas, electrics) run.
  3. Flag what's fixed vs flexible — mark walls you can't move and anything you're open to changing.
  4. Add a short note — list your priorities, budget range, and any decisions still open.
  5. Send it early — attach the plan to your first message so the quote reflects reality.

Worked example: Sarah is replacing her galley kitchen. She measures the room, draws it in a free 2D planner, and places her wanted units to scale. The plan shows the fridge would block the door swing. She shifts it on screen, re-exports the PNG, and emails it to two fitters with a note: "Ceiling 2.4m, waste pipe on the left wall, budget around 6k, open on worktop material." Both fitters quote from the same accurate layout — no site visit needed to start, and no surprise about the fridge.

That's the payoff: fewer misunderstandings, tighter quotes, and no expensive rework.

Frequently asked questions

Quick answers to the questions renovators ask most about room planning tools.

Will my contractor accept a plan made in a free tool?

Yes. Most contractors welcome any clear, scaled plan because it saves them guesswork and reduces costly mistakes. What matters is accuracy and readability, not the software's price tag. A clean plan with real measurements and marked services is far more useful to a fitter than a hand sketch or a verbal description.

What should I annotate before sending to a professional?

Mark all wall lengths, ceiling height, and door and window positions. Show where water, gas, and electrical services run, and note which walls are fixed. Add your budget range and any decisions still open. These details let a professional quote accurately without a wasted site visit.

Do free room planning tools need a download or sign-up?

Some do, some don't. Heavier 3D and CAD tools usually need an account or install. Browser-based 2D planners often let you start drawing immediately with no sign-up or download — the fastest route if you just need a plan to share.

Are 3D room planners worth the extra effort?

They're worth it when you want to preview colours, materials, and furniture in context. For getting an accurate layout to a contractor, a 2D plan is quicker and easier to read. Many renovators draw in 2D first, then use 3D only for final visual decisions.

Can free tools handle a whole-house floor plan?

Many can, though free tiers sometimes cap the number of rooms or projects. For multi-room plans, check the limit before you start. Drawing room by room and combining them is often the simplest approach for a full home layout.

Do these tools include a furniture catalogue?

Most 3D tools do — Planner 5D, RoomStyler, and Autodesk Homestyler all offer large furniture catalogues you can drag into your room. Brand planners like IKEA's use their own product range. 2D planners focus on scaled shapes so you can check fit rather than browse specific products.
