What Makes a Free Room Planner Worth Using?

Not every tool that calls itself free actually is. Before ranking these tools, here are the five criteria used to evaluate each one:

  • No account or download required — you should be able to open it and start planning immediately
  • Snap-to-grid accuracy and live measurements — guesswork leads to expensive mistakes with contractors
  • Ability to add furniture and fixtures — essential for bathroom and kitchen planning
  • Export or share the finished plan — a plan you can't send to your fitter is a plan that doesn't help you
  • Works for specific rooms — bathroom, kitchen, and bedroom planners need room-specific fixture libraries

2. RoomStyler 3D Home Planner

RoomStyler is strong on visual output. You can build a room in 2D and then switch to a 3D walkthrough view, which is genuinely useful when you want to see how a bathroom remodel will feel rather than just how it measures.

The furniture library is large, and the interface is relatively intuitive for a browser-based tool.

The honest catch: Saving your plan requires a free account. If you close the tab without saving, your work is gone. That's a real friction point if you're mid-session and get interrupted.

Best for: Users who want a 3D visualisation of a finished room and don't mind creating an account to save their progress.

Not ideal for: Quickly briefing a contractor with accurate measurements — the 3D focus makes it less precise for technical layout work.

3. Planner 5D

Planner 5D has the most polished interface of any free tool in this list. The drag-and-drop system is smooth, there's a mobile app available, and the visual quality of the output is noticeably higher than browser-only alternatives.

The free tier gives you access to core features and a basic furniture library. The 3D render quality on the free plan is limited compared to the paid version, but it's still usable for early-stage planning.

The honest catch: Sign-up is required. The free tier also restricts how many projects you can save and limits access to some fixture libraries — which matters when you're planning a bathroom and need specific sanitaryware options.

Best for: Users who want a premium-feeling tool and are happy to create an account. Also a strong pick for anyone planning on mobile.

Not ideal for: Fast, no-friction contractor briefing or users who prefer not to register.

4. SmartDraw Free Floor Plan Tool

SmartDraw produces professional-grade output and handles multi-room floor plans well. If you're a contractor or architect who needs to create detailed layouts for client presentations, it has the feature depth to match.

The honest catch: It's not truly free. SmartDraw operates on a trial model — you get limited access without paying, but full functionality requires a subscription. It also requires both a download and an account, which puts it outside the criteria for this list.

Best for: Contractors and professionals who need detailed, multi-room plans and are willing to pay for a subscription tool.

Not ideal for: Homeowners who want a free room planner online no sign up — this tool doesn't meet that standard.

How to Get the Most Out of Any Free Room Planner

The tool matters less than the preparation. These four habits apply regardless of which planner you pick:

  1. Measure the room before you open the tool. Write down the length and width of every wall, plus the position and size of windows and doors. Accurate input gives you an accurate plan. For help with this step, the room measurement tips guide walks through the full process.
  2. Use snap-to-grid. Every tool above has some form of grid alignment. Use it. Freehand placement leads to small errors that compound — a 5cm mistake in a bathroom plan can mean a vanity unit that doesn't fit.
  3. Place fixtures before furniture. In a bathroom plan, lock in the toilet, bath or shower, and basin positions first. Everything else works around the plumbing.
  4. Export and share before your first contractor meeting. A clean floor plan changes the quality of that conversation. Your fitter can spot problems on paper that would cost significantly more to fix on site.
