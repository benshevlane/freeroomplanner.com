Why Planning Your Kitchen Layout Matters Before Buying Units

B&Q offers an impressive range of kitchen units across several collections — from budget-friendly options to premium shaker styles. But even the most stylish units won't save you if the layout doesn't work. Poor kitchen planning is one of the most common and costly DIY mistakes homeowners make.

Here's what can go wrong when you skip the planning stage:

  • Units that don't fit: Standard B&Q kitchen units come in specific widths (typically 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 1000mm). Without a proper plan, you might find you have an awkward 150mm gap that no standard unit will fill.
  • Appliance conflicts: Ovens, fridges, dishwashers, and washing machines all need dedicated spaces. Forgetting to account for these can leave you with nowhere to put them.
  • Poor workflow: The classic kitchen work triangle — the relationship between your sink, hob, and fridge — affects how functional your kitchen feels every single day.
  • Wasted wall space: Without planning, it's easy to miss opportunities for tall larder units, wine racks, or corner solutions.
  • Budget overruns: Over-ordering units (or having to re-order because something doesn't fit) costs both time and money.

A proper floor plan solves all of these problems before they happen. That's exactly what our free kitchen planner is designed to help you do.

Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

Before you start drawing your layout, it helps to understand the standard dimensions that B&Q kitchen units are built around. Most UK kitchen units — including those from B&Q — follow standard sizing conventions.

Base Units (Floor Cabinets)

  • Height: Typically 720mm (excluding worktop), designed so that with a standard worktop and plinth, the finished height is around 900mm
  • Depth: Usually 560mm or 600mm
  • Width: Available in 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, 800mm, and 1000mm widths

Wall Units (Upper Cabinets)

  • Height: Commonly 576mm, 720mm, or 900mm
  • Depth: Usually 300mm
  • Width: Mirrors base unit widths (300mm–1000mm)

Tall Units (Larder and Oven Housing)

  • Height: 1970mm or 2150mm
  • Depth: 560mm or 600mm
  • Width: Typically 300mm, 500mm, or 600mm

Understanding these dimensions means you can use our kitchen planner with real accuracy — drawing your room to scale and placing units that reflect the actual products you plan to buy.

How to Measure Your Kitchen for B&Q Units

Accurate measurements are the foundation of any successful kitchen plan. Here's how to do it properly:

  1. Measure the overall room dimensions: Length and width of the room, measured at floor level. Measure in at least two places (walls can bow slightly).
  2. Mark all doors and windows: Note their exact position from each corner, and record the width of each opening. Don't forget the direction doors swing — this affects which units you can place nearby.
  3. Locate fixed services: Mark where your gas point, water supply, waste pipe, and electrical sockets are positioned. These are the hardest things to move and will heavily influence your layout.
  4. Measure ceiling height: Important if you're considering tall units or if you want wall units to reach the ceiling.
  5. Check for obstacles: Note any radiators, boiler cupboards, or structural pillars.

Planning Your Kitchen Layout Around B&Q Units

With your measurements in hand, it's time to think about layout. There are several classic kitchen configurations that work well with standard unit sizing:

Galley Kitchen

Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Works brilliantly in narrow rooms. Base units of 600mm depth on both sides require a minimum walkway width of 900mm between them — 1200mm or more is ideal if two people cook together.

L-Shaped Kitchen

Units run along two adjacent walls, forming an L. This is one of the most popular layouts for medium-sized kitchens. A corner unit (such as a B&Q corner base with a carousel) is needed where the two runs meet.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls — excellent for maximising storage in a larger room. You'll need two corner solutions and a minimum room width of around 2.4 metres to allow comfortable movement.

Island Kitchen

A freestanding central island adds prep space and storage, and can house a hob or sink. B&Q stocks island units specifically designed for this purpose. You'll need at least 900mm–1200mm of clear floor space around all sides of the island.

The Work Triangle: Getting Your Layout Right

The kitchen work triangle is a time-tested design principle that positions the three most-used workstations — the sink, hob, and fridge — in a triangular arrangement. When these three points are too far apart, cooking becomes tiring. When they're too close, the kitchen feels cramped.

The recommended guidelines are:

  • Each side of the triangle should be between 1.2 and 2.7 metres
  • The total perimeter of the triangle should be between 4 and 8 metres
  • No single leg of the triangle should be blocked by a door, island, or heavy foot traffic

When placing your B&Q kitchen units in the planner, keep the work triangle in mind. Place your sink unit first (near the window if possible, and close to the waste pipe), then position your hob housing, then your fridge space — adjusting until the triangle feels balanced.

Don't Forget These Often-Overlooked Details

Even experienced renovators can miss these planning essentials:

  • Plinth height: B&Q units typically sit on legs that are hidden by a plinth panel. Factor in the total unit height including plinth when checking clearance below wall units.
  • Worktop overhang: Standard worktops overhang the unit by 40mm at the front. This matters when positioning units near doorways.
  • Extraction route: Your cooker hood needs to vent somewhere — either through an external wall or via a recirculation filter. Plan the ducting route before finalising hob placement.
  • Dishwasher and washing machine placement: These need to be close to water supply and waste. Placing them far from your sink usually means costly plumbing alterations.
  • Bin storage: Pull-out bin units are popular with B&Q kitchens — they typically take up a 400mm or 600mm base unit space. Plan for them specifically.
  • Lighting: Under-cabinet LED lighting is easiest to install when planned from the start, as wiring can be hidden during the fit.

From Floor Plan to B&Q Shopping List

One of the most practical benefits of planning your kitchen properly is that your floor plan directly translates into your shopping list. Once your layout is finalised in the planner, you can note down every unit by its width and type:

  1. List each base unit with its width (e.g., 600mm base unit with drawer, 500mm base unit with door)
  2. List each wall unit with height and width
  3. Note any tall units (larder, oven housing, fridge housing)
  4. Add corner units where needed
  5. Account for fillers, end panels, and plinths
  6. Add worktops — measure the total linear run and add extra for mitres and joints

With this list, you can shop confidently on the B&Q website or in-store, knowing exactly what you need. You're also in a much better position if you're using a kitchen fitter — sharing your floor plan means they can quote accurately and spot any potential issues before work begins.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sizes do B&Q kitchen units come in?

B&Q kitchen units follow standard UK sizing. Base units are typically 720mm tall (excluding worktop and plinth) and 560–600mm deep, with widths ranging from 300mm to 1000mm. Wall units are usually 300mm deep and available in heights of 576mm, 720mm, or 900mm. Tall units reach 1970mm or 2150mm in height. These standard dimensions mean they're easy to plan with a scaled floor planner.

Can I plan my kitchen online before visiting B&Q?

Absolutely. In fact, arriving at B&Q with a completed floor plan is highly recommended. It saves time, helps B&Q staff give you accurate advice, and means you won't over- or under-order units. You can use our free kitchen planner to draw your room to scale and experiment with different layouts before your visit — no sign-up required.

Do I need a professional to fit B&Q kitchen units?

Many homeowners fit B&Q kitchens themselves, particularly the flat-pack ranges. However, most people hire a kitchen fitter for at least part of the job — especially for gas connection, electrical work, and tiling. Having a detailed floor plan makes the process smoother whether you're fitting it yourself or briefing a professional.

How do I account for corner units in my kitchen plan?

Corner units are one of the trickiest parts of kitchen planning. B&Q offers corner base units with internal carousels or pull-out solutions. When planning, the corner itself typically takes up 900mm–1000mm on each wall (measured from the corner). Make sure your plan accounts for this space so that the adjoining units align correctly and doors can open without clashing.
