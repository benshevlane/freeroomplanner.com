Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

One of the first things to get your head around is that kitchen units — whether from B&Q or any other retailer — follow a fairly standardised sizing system. Understanding this system makes it much easier to plan your layout accurately.

Standard Base Unit Dimensions

Base units (the floor-standing cupboards) in the B&Q range typically come in the following widths:

  • 300mm (30cm) — narrow units, often used for pull-out storage or as fillers
  • 400mm (40cm) — useful in tighter spaces
  • 500mm (50cm) — a common mid-size for drawers and cupboards
  • 600mm (60cm) — the most popular width, used for standard cupboards and sink units
  • 1000mm (100cm) — wide units, often used for corner solutions or large drawer sets

Standard base unit height (without worktop) is typically 870mm, and depth is usually 570mm (with doors sitting flush to around 600mm). When planning, always account for the worktop on top — typically adding 40mm to the overall height.

Standard Wall Unit Dimensions

Wall units are mounted above the worktop and base units. Common widths mirror those of the base units (300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm), while heights typically range from:

  • 360mm — shorter wall units, often used above a fridge or microwave
  • 570mm — mid-height, popular in lower-ceiling kitchens
  • 720mm — taller wall units for maximum storage
  • 900mm — floor-to-ceiling or very tall units

Wall unit depth is typically around 300mm, which is shallower than base units to avoid hitting your head while working at the worktop.

Tall and Tower Units

Tall units — often used for larder storage, integrated fridges, or ovens — are generally 2100mm or 2200mm tall and either 300mm, 500mm, or 600mm wide. These are worth planning in early as they often anchor one end of a run of units.

How to Measure Your Kitchen Correctly

Before you can plan a kitchen layout or start thinking about which B&Q kitchen units to choose, you need accurate room measurements. Rushing this step leads to expensive mistakes.

What You'll Need

  • A steel tape measure (not a fabric one — they stretch)
  • A pencil and paper, or better yet, a digital tool to record measurements
  • A helper to hold one end of the tape if the room is large

Step-by-Step Measuring Guide

  1. Measure the overall room dimensions — length and width at floor level, at multiple points (walls aren't always perfectly straight).
  2. Mark the position of doors and windows — measure from each corner to the edge of the door or window frame, and record the width of the opening.
  3. Note the position of radiators and boilers — these will affect where units can go.
  4. Identify the location of water supply and waste pipes — your sink will need to be near existing plumbing unless you're prepared for costly rerouting.
  5. Record ceiling height — especially important if you're considering tall units or ceiling-height wall cabinets.
  6. Check for any alcoves, chimney breasts, or irregular walls — these will need special unit configurations or custom panels.

Popular Kitchen Layouts and How B&Q Units Fit Into Them

The layout of your kitchen will largely be determined by the shape of your room, but understanding the standard configurations will help you choose and arrange your B&Q kitchen units more effectively.

Galley Kitchen (Single or Double Run)

A galley kitchen runs units along one or both parallel walls. This is a highly efficient layout for cooking, as everything is within easy reach. When planning a galley layout, pay close attention to the aisle width between facing units — a minimum of 1000mm is recommended for comfortable movement, and 1200mm is better if two people will use the kitchen simultaneously.

L-Shaped Kitchen

One of the most popular layouts for open-plan living. Units run along two adjacent walls, forming an L-shape. Corner units — such as carousel corner units or Le Mans pull-outs — are essential here to make the most of the corner space. B&Q stocks several corner unit solutions worth exploring.

U-Shaped Kitchen

Units on three walls, creating a highly functional workspace. Great for larger kitchens. Two corner units are required, and careful planning is needed to ensure the corners don't create awkward, unusable space.

Island Kitchen

An island can be added to any of the above layouts, provided there is enough floor space. As a rule of thumb, you need at least 900mm to 1000mm of clear floor space on all sides of the island for comfortable movement. Islands are often made from base units — B&Q sells specific island-configuration units — and can incorporate a hob, sink, or simply extra worktop and storage.

The Kitchen Work Triangle — and Why It Still Matters

The work triangle is a classic kitchen design principle that refers to the relationship between your three most-used areas: the hob, sink, and fridge. For an efficient kitchen, these three points should form a triangle with each side measuring between 1.2m and 2.7m, and the total of all three sides shouldn't exceed 8m.

When arranging your B&Q kitchen units, keep the work triangle in mind. Poor placement of these key appliances — for example, putting the fridge on the opposite side of the room from the hob — can make the kitchen feel exhausting to work in, regardless of how beautiful the units look.

Tips for Choosing the Right B&Q Kitchen Units

With dozens of kitchen ranges available — from budget-friendly flatpack styles to more premium shaker and handleless designs — here are some practical tips for making the right choice:

  • Start with your layout, not your style. Know exactly what units you need before falling in love with a door colour or handle style. A beautiful kitchen that doesn't function well is a daily frustration.
  • Prioritise storage where you need it most. Deep drawer packs near the hob are ideal for pots and pans. A tall larder unit near the fridge keeps food organised. Plan storage around how you actually cook.
  • Don't forget filler panels and end panels. These are the unsung heroes of a professional-looking kitchen fit. They cover gaps at the end of runs and between units and walls.
  • Consider internal organisers. Soft-close hinges, pull-out bins, cutlery trays, and pan dividers can dramatically improve how usable your kitchen feels day-to-day.
  • Check appliance dimensions before finalising your unit plan. Built-in ovens, dishwashers, washing machines, and integrated fridges all have specific housing requirements. Always check the spec sheet before ordering units to house them.
  • Allow for services. Leave space for electrical sockets, gas pipes, and plumbing — especially around the sink and hob areas.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sizes do B&Q kitchen units come in?

B&Q kitchen units follow standard UK sizing conventions. Base unit widths typically range from 300mm to 1000mm, with 600mm being the most common. Wall units come in similar widths and heights ranging from 360mm to 900mm. Tall and tower units are usually 2100mm or 2200mm in height. Always check the specific dimensions on the B&Q product listing before planning, as sizes can vary slightly between ranges.

Can I plan my kitchen layout before visiting B&Q?

Absolutely — and it's strongly recommended. Arriving at a store (or speaking to an online kitchen advisor) with a scaled floor plan and a clear list of units already makes the entire process smoother and more accurate. You can use our free kitchen planner to draw your room and experiment with different layouts before you shop.

How do I know how many units I need?

Start by drawing your room to scale and identifying where units can go (along walls, avoiding doors, windows, radiators, and boilers). Then fill the available wall runs with units, working from the corners outward. Count up your base units, wall units, and tall units separately, and don't forget corner units, filler panels, and end panels. A digital planner makes this much easier to visualise and adjust.

Do I need a professional kitchen designer to plan a B&Q kitchen?

Not necessarily. Many homeowners successfully plan and install B&Q kitchens themselves or with the help of a local fitter. B&Q also offers a paid kitchen design service if you want professional guidance. However, using a free planning tool to create your initial layout gives you a solid foundation — whether you take it to a professional designer or proceed independently.

What is the best kitchen layout for a small kitchen?

For small kitchens, a galley layout (units along one or both parallel walls) or an L-shaped layout tends to be the most space-efficient. Maximise storage with tall units and full-height wall cabinets. Avoid islands unless the room is genuinely large enough — cramming one in will make the space feel smaller and restrict movement.
