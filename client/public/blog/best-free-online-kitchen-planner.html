What Makes a Free Kitchen Planner Worth Using?

Not all free tools are equal. Before you spend twenty minutes in a planner only to hit a paywall at export, check these five criteria.

  • No sign-up required. You should be able to open the tool and start drawing immediately.
  • No download. Browser-based means it works on any device without installing anything.
  • Accurate measurements. A plan is useless to a contractor if the dimensions are guesswork.
  • Appliances and units included. You need to place a fridge, cooker, sink, and cabinets — not just draw walls.
  • Exportable output. A shareable image or PDF is the whole point. If you can't get your plan out of the tool, it hasn't saved you any time.

Every tool below was evaluated against these five criteria.

3. RoomSketcher

RoomSketcher has a clean interface and handles wall drawing well. The floor plan mode is intuitive, and the furniture library is broad enough to cover most kitchen layouts. According to RoomSketcher's own documentation, users can create 2D and 3D floor plans in the same project.

How to Plan Your Kitchen Layout in 5 Steps

Any free planner works better when you arrive with accurate measurements and a rough idea of what you want. Follow these steps.

  1. Measure the room. Record every wall length, plus the position and width of doors, windows, and any fixed points like a boiler or soil pipe. Double-check everything — a 10cm error throws off an entire layout.
  2. Note door swing and window positions. These dictate where units can and can't go. A wall cabinet that blocks a door is a mistake that costs money to fix.
  3. Choose a layout type. Galley, L-shape, U-shape, or island — pick the one that suits your room size before you start placing units.
  4. Add appliances and units. Work from fixed points outward. Place the sink near existing plumbing, the cooker on an appropriate wall, and build your storage around them.
  5. Export and share. Download your floor plan as an image and send it to your fitter before the first meeting. It saves time, reduces miscommunication, and gives you something concrete to refine together.

For more detail on getting your measurements right before you start, the room measurement tips guide covers every step.

The 4 Most Common Kitchen Layout Types

Galley: Two parallel runs of units facing each other. Works well in narrow kitchens. Efficient workflow but can feel tight with more than one person cooking.

L-shape: Units along two adjacent walls. Suits medium-sized rooms and works well when one wall opens to a dining area. Flexible and popular in open-plan homes.

U-shape: Units on three walls. Maximum storage and worktop space. Best in larger kitchens — in a small room, a U-shape can feel enclosed.

Island: An L-shape or U-shape with a freestanding unit in the centre. Needs enough floor space to allow at least 90cm of clear walkway on all sides. Adds worktop space and a social focal point.

Tips for Getting a Plan Your Contractor Can Actually Use

A floor plan is only useful if your contractor can read it. Keep these points in mind before you export.

  • Include all dimensions on the exported plan, not just the overall room size.
  • Mark the current position of plumbing and electrical points — moving them adds significant cost.
  • Note ceiling height if you're planning tall units or an extractor that needs ducting.
  • Use a tool that exports as an image or PDF so the file is easy to send and open on any device.
  • Add a note about which walls are load-bearing if you know — this gives your contractor useful context before the site visit.

If you're planning other rooms at the same time, the approach is similar across spaces. The principles that apply to a kitchen island layout transfer directly to any room where you're working around a fixed central element.

Mistakes to Avoid When Using a Free Kitchen Planner

  1. Starting without measurements. Guessing room dimensions and adjusting later wastes time and produces a plan nobody can rely on.
  2. Forgetting door swing clearance. A door that swings into a unit or appliance is a problem you want to catch on screen, not on installation day.
  3. Ignoring the work triangle. The path between your fridge, sink, and cooker should be clear and short. Layouts that ignore this feel inefficient to use every day.
  4. Using a retailer-specific tool before choosing a supplier. If you design in IKEA's planner and then switch to a local kitchen maker, you're starting from scratch.
  5. Picking a tool that can't export. If you can't get the plan out, it's a dead end. Check export options before you invest time in any tool.
