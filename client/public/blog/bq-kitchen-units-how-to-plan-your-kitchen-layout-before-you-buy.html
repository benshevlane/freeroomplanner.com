B&Q Kitchen Units: How to Plan Your Kitchen Layout Before You Buy

Why Planning Before You Buy B&Q Kitchen Units Matters

Buying a new kitchen is one of the biggest investments a homeowner can make. B&Q kitchen units are a popular choice across the UK, offering a wide range of styles, sizes, and price points — from their affordable GoodHome range through to more premium fitted kitchen options. But here's the thing: even the best quality units won't save a kitchen that has been poorly planned.

Before you visit a store, speak to a kitchen designer, or add a single item to your basket, it pays to have a clear visual plan of your kitchen layout. That means knowing exactly where your base units, wall units, tall larder units, and appliances will sit — and how they'll flow together in the space you actually have.

Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes and Ranges

One of the first things you'll notice when browsing B&Q kitchen units is that they come in a standardised set of widths and heights. Understanding these dimensions before you start planning is essential, because your floor plan needs to accommodate them accurately.

Standard Base Unit Widths

B&Q base units are typically available in the following widths:

  • 300mm — ideal for narrow spaces or filler panels
  • 400mm — useful for smaller kitchens or awkward corners
  • 500mm — a versatile mid-size unit
  • 600mm — the most common standard width
  • 800mm — often used for sink units or double drawer packs
  • 1000mm — wider base units for maximising storage

Standard base unit height is typically 720mm before worktop, and standard depth is 560mm (to allow for a 600mm worktop overhang). Wall units are generally 300mm deep and come in heights of 360mm, 600mm, or 720mm depending on the range.

Common B&Q Kitchen Ranges

B&Q currently stock several kitchen door styles and cabinet ranges. Some of the most popular include:

  • GoodHome Stevia — a clean, minimal handle-free style
  • GoodHome Atomia — freestanding modular units with a contemporary finish
  • GoodHome Caraway — a classic shaker-style door in various colours
  • Marletti — a premium painted finish range

Knowing which range you're leaning towards will affect not just aesthetics but also which unit sizes are available, so it's worth checking the B&Q website or visiting your local store to confirm dimensions before finalising your plan.

Step 1: Draw Your Kitchen Walls to Scale

Start by accurately measuring your kitchen. You'll need:

  • The overall room dimensions (length and width)
  • The position and width of any doors and windows
  • The location of existing plumbing (sink, dishwasher connections)
  • The position of any gas or electric points
  • Any alcoves, chimney breasts, or awkward angles

Step 2: Plan Your Kitchen Layout Type

Before placing any units, decide on the overall layout shape that suits your room. Common options include:

  1. Galley kitchen — units along two parallel walls, ideal for narrow rooms
  2. L-shaped kitchen — units along two adjacent walls, great for maximising corner storage
  3. U-shaped kitchen — units along three walls, offering maximum storage and worktop space
  4. Island kitchen — an L or U-shape with a central island unit for extra prep space
  5. Single-wall kitchen — all units along one wall, common in open-plan spaces

Each layout has implications for how your B&Q kitchen units will be arranged, where your appliances sit, and how traffic flows through the space. The classic kitchen work triangle principle — keeping the sink, hob, and fridge in a triangular relationship — is a useful guide regardless of your layout type.

Planning Tips Specific to B&Q Kitchen Installations

Account for Filler Panels and End Panels

Rarely does a kitchen run fit perfectly wall-to-wall with standard unit widths. B&Q sell filler panels and end panels precisely because gaps are almost inevitable. When planning your layout, measure the total wall length and work out how your chosen unit widths will add up. If you have a 3200mm run and you're using 600mm units, you'll have a 400mm gap to account for — either with a 400mm unit, a combination of smaller units, or filler panels at one or both ends.

Plan for Your Appliances Accurately

Integrated appliances — particularly dishwashers, ovens, and fridge-freezers — have specific housing requirements. A standard integrated appliance opening is typically 600mm wide and 720mm tall. If you're planning to use B&Q's own-brand or third-party integrated appliances, double-check the cut-out dimensions and ensure your floor plan reflects them accurately.

Don't Forget the Plumbing and Electrics

Your floor plan should indicate where the water supply and waste pipes currently sit, as moving these adds significant cost to any kitchen project. Similarly, note the positions of existing electrical sockets and, crucially, any fixed gas points. Your B&Q kitchen units will need to work around these — or your plan needs to budget for the cost of moving them.

Think About Tall Units and Wall Units Together

A common oversight is planning base units without considering how wall units and tall larder units will interact above them. Standard wall units sit roughly 450–500mm above the worktop surface. If you have low ceilings or high windows, some wall unit heights may not be suitable. Plan the full vertical elevation — not just the floor plan — to ensure everything fits.

From Plan to Purchase: A Suggested Workflow

If you're planning a kitchen renovation using B&Q kitchen units, here's a sensible order of operations to follow:

  1. Measure your room accurately — use a laser measure if possible for precision
  2. Draw your floor plan using the free kitchen planner at freeroomplanner.com
  3. Experiment with layouts — try at least two different configurations before settling
  4. Research B&Q unit ranges — check sizes, door styles, and availability online or in-store
  5. Refine your plan to match the exact unit widths you intend to buy
  6. Export your plan as PNG and share with your kitchen fitter for quoting
  7. Visit B&Q (in-store or online) to order your units, worktops, and accessories
  8. Book your fitter with a confirmed start date that aligns with your unit delivery

This workflow helps avoid the frustrating and expensive scenario of ordering units that don't fit, or booking a fitter before your materials have arrived.
