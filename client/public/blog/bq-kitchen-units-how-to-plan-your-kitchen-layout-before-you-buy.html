Understanding B&Q Kitchen Unit Sizes

Before you start placing units, it helps to understand the standard dimensions that B&Q — and most UK kitchen manufacturers — work to. Knowing these measurements means you can plan your layout accurately and check everything fits before ordering.

Base Unit Dimensions

  • Height: Typically 720mm (before worktop), rising to around 870–900mm with worktop fitted
  • Depth: Standard 570mm (to allow for a 600mm worktop with a small overhang)
  • Width: Most commonly 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm, and 1000mm (for corner units)

Wall Unit Dimensions

  • Height: Usually 360mm, 570mm, 720mm, or 900mm depending on ceiling height and style
  • Depth: Typically 300mm
  • Width: Mirrors base unit widths — 300mm, 400mm, 500mm, 600mm

Tall Units (Larder and Appliance Housing)

  • Height: 1970mm or 2150mm
  • Depth: 570mm or 300mm depending on type
  • Width: Usually 300mm, 500mm, or 600mm

Step-by-Step: How to Plan Your Kitchen Layout Online

Once you have your room measurements written down, you're ready to start planning. Here's a simple process you can follow using the free kitchen planner tool.

  1. Measure your kitchen accurately. Note the overall room dimensions, but also record the positions of doors, windows, radiators, soil pipes, and any fixed appliances like boilers or extractor ducting. Measure in centimetres for the most useful level of detail.
  2. Draw your walls. Use the room planner to draw the outline of your kitchen. The snap-to-grid feature works in 10cm increments, making it easy to input standard room dimensions. Add openings for doors and windows where they sit in the real room.
  3. Add your appliances first. Position your sink (which must sit near the soil stack), your hob (ideally under or near the extractor position), and your fridge. These are your fixed points — everything else builds around them.
  4. Place your base units. Begin filling in runs of base units around the walls. Use the resize handles to match B&Q's standard widths. Pay close attention to corner solutions — L-shaped and U-shaped kitchens need corner units or carousels, and these take up more space than people expect.
  5. Add wall units. Position overhead storage above your base unit runs. Leave the space above your hob clear for your extractor hood. Leave gaps above your sink if you have a window there.
  6. Check your workflow triangle. The classic kitchen design principle places your fridge, sink, and hob at three points of a triangle with clear, unobstructed paths between them. Check this on your plan before you finalise anything.
  7. Export your floor plan. When you're happy with the layout, export it as a PNG image. You can print this, share it with a kitchen fitter, or take it to the B&Q kitchen planning desk for a quote.

The Most Popular B&Q Kitchen Layouts — and How to Plan Each One

Galley Kitchen Layout

A galley kitchen runs units along two parallel walls, creating a narrow corridor. It's highly efficient and works well in smaller homes or open-plan spaces where the kitchen occupies one end of a room.

When planning a galley layout with B&Q kitchen units, ensure there's at least 1200mm between the two facing unit runs. Anything narrower and two people can't work comfortably side by side, and dishwasher or oven doors may obstruct the walkway when open.

L-Shaped Kitchen Layout

One of the most popular layouts in UK homes, the L-shape runs units along two adjacent walls. It creates a natural work triangle and leaves the rest of the room open — ideal if you want a dining area in the same space.

The corner junction is the critical measurement here. B&Q offer blind corner base units and corner carousel options. Draw these carefully in your floor plan — a standard corner unit typically occupies around 900–1000mm on both sides of the corner.

U-Shaped Kitchen Layout

Units on three walls create a U-shape, maximising storage and worktop space. This layout suits larger kitchens and works brilliantly for keen cooks who need everything within reach.

You'll need two corner solutions (one at each inside corner), so plan these carefully. Also check that the open end of the U is wide enough — you'll want at least 1200mm between opposing unit runs, preferably 1500mm or more.

Single-Wall Kitchen Layout

All units run along one wall only. Common in studio flats, narrow rooms, or as part of a larger open-plan living space. When using B&Q kitchen units in a single-wall arrangement, prioritise tall units at the ends to maximise storage vertically, and consider a kitchen island opposite if space allows.

Common Kitchen Planning Mistakes to Avoid

Experienced kitchen fitters see the same errors again and again. Here are the most common planning mistakes — all of which show up clearly when you work through a scaled floor plan.

  • Not accounting for door swings. An oven door that opens into a dishwasher door, or a larder door that can't open fully because of a kitchen island — these clashes are invisible on a shopping list but obvious on a floor plan.
  • Ignoring the plumbing stack position. Your sink must drain into the soil stack. If you position it on the wrong wall, you'll face expensive re-routing or visible pipe runs. Note where the existing waste pipe exits when you draw your plan.
  • Placing the hob directly under a window. Extractor hoods need to be fixed above the hob, typically 650–750mm above it. A window in this position makes hood installation complicated or impossible.
  • Underestimating appliance depths. A standard fridge-freezer can be 600–700mm deep. If you're planning integrated appliances into B&Q kitchen units, double-check the internal cabinet depth against your appliance's specifications.
  • Forgetting the bin. It sounds trivial, but a full-size pull-out bin unit takes up 300–400mm of base unit width. Plan for it deliberately, ideally near the prep area and sink.

Tips for Getting Accurate Measurements Before You Plan

A floor plan is only as useful as the measurements that underpin it. Before you open the kitchen planner, take the time to measure your room properly.

  • Use a laser measure for room dimensions — it's faster and more accurate than a tape measure for distances over 2 metres
  • Measure the height of the room as well as the floor plan, particularly if you're planning tall units to ceiling height
  • Note the position and width of every door and window, and measure from each corner rather than assuming positions are symmetric
  • Mark the position of electrical sockets, light switches, and extractor ducting — these may limit where certain units can go
  • Record the position of the boiler, any radiators, and the stop cock
  • Measure the height and width of any alcoves or chimney breasts that might affect unit placement
